## Stop watching. Start writing.

Every other SQL course makes you **watch** slides for two hours before you touch a keyboard. Not here. In the next minute **you** write a real query and get a real answer — then we'll explain what just happened.

## A real question

Here's how SQL actually starts in a job. Your boss leans over and asks:

> *How many orders did we ship in June?*

Most people open a spreadsheet and start scrolling. An hour later, they're still scrolling. You're not going to **find** the answer — you're going to **ask** for it.

## Your first query

Type this exactly (don't copy-paste — typing is how it sticks), and hit **Run**:

```sql
SELECT count(*) AS june_orders
FROM "Orders"
WHERE "OrderDate" >= DATE '2024-06-01'
  AND "OrderDate" <  DATE '2024-07-01';
```

```text
 june_orders
-------------
           4
```

**Four.** One line, and you just answered a real business question from real company data. That is SQL. Read it the way you'd say it out loud: *count the rows in Orders where the order date falls in June.* (`count(*)` counts rows; `WHERE` keeps only the ones you care about; the half-open range `>= June 1 … < July 1` captures all of June with no end-of-month surprises.)

## It's real data, not toy data

No `foo`, no `bar`. Real customers, real countries — ask who they are:

```sql
SELECT "CompanyName", "Country"
FROM "Customers"
LIMIT 5;
```

```text
 CompanyName                          | Country
--------------------------------------+---------
 Alfreds Futterkiste                  | Germany
 Ana Trujillo Emparedados y helados   | Mexico
 Antonio Moreno Taquería              | Mexico
 Around the Horn                      | UK
 Berglunds snabbköp                   | Sweden
```

This is **Northwind** — a small import/export company (Customers, Orders, Products, Suppliers, Employees). Real enough to ask real questions, small enough to fit in your head.

## One skill, every database

We practise on **PostgreSQL** — it's free and the cleanest teacher there is. But what you learn here runs on Oracle, SQL Server, MySQL and Db2 too. Learn it **once**; whenever a query needs a vendor-specific trick, we'll flag it and show the portable version. You're not learning one database — you're learning SQL.

## The deal: how every lesson works

1. **Type** the query yourself — typing teaches your brain; copy-paste only teaches your eyes.
2. **Predict** the answer before you run it. Say it out loud: *"I expect 4."* Wrong → you just learned something. Right → that's earned confidence. (Our **koans** turn this into code: fill in the number, run, go green.)
3. **Run** it. No watching. No slides you forget by lunch.

Do this for a handful of lessons and you'll answer questions nobody else at your company can.
